§ 01 · What I build

What we ship.
AI sessions + human review.

Three layers, one loop. L1 Auto-research — agents mutate their own prompts in the background; AI judges score variants against synthetic fixtures; winners replace incumbents. Inspired by Karpathy autoresearch and adjacent self-improving systems. L2 Engineer tuning — we pick which auto-research variant powers each client's production agents. L3 Production — agents draft, engineer + human review every output, work ships. The loop closes at the top: reviewer signals from production feed back into auto-research as new fixtures, so L1 keeps improving against real-world judgment — not just synthetic tests.

01 · Text

Content factories.

Long-form articles + blog SEO. Short-form posts on X + LinkedIn. Email + newsletter copy. Same voice persona consumed by every channel — no rewriting per surface.

Outputs: daily-to-weekly cadence · ranked drafts with reviewer notes · what shipped + why.
02 · Visual + video

Creative production.

Static visuals (IG carousels, LinkedIn document carousels, hero images, ad statics). Short-form video for TikTok, Reels, Stories. Paid ad creative for Meta + LinkedIn.

Outputs: AI-generated previews · scene plans · voice scripts · review-ready before ship.
03 · Signals

Intelligence + monitoring.

Continuous scan of competitors, brand mentions, AI-search readiness, share-of-voice. Ad libraries, news, social, Reddit, podcasts, reviews, search. Ranked signals — not a dashboard of noise.

Outputs: weekly digest · real-time competitor alerts · monthly market briefs.
04 · Visibility

Diagnostics + reporting.

149-lens marketing audit (the $1K–$3K phase 01). Live portal: agent transcripts, cost ledger, reviewer events, audit trail. Compliance gating where your industry requires it.

Outputs: HTML reports · raw JSONL · cost-per-output ledger · full audit trail.

Custom pipeline not on this list? Tell me what work needs doing. We build new lanes when clients ask — quote and timeline scale with scope. The audit decides which standard ones you need; usually a mix.

§ 05 · Built for

Three audience shapes.
One engine behind all of them.

Distribution looks different depending on who's hunting whom. The engine doesn't change — the pipelines on it do. If your audience falls in any of these shapes, the architecture maps.

Engineer-buyer audiences

B2B software · AI · infra.

Buyers who research before they ever talk to sales. Read the docs, compare the diffs, test the demo. Long-form technical content, comparison pieces, share-of-voice tracking, competitor launch alerts, search-rank defense in a category they already know is hot.

Commerce + consumer audiences

E-commerce · DTC · content brands.

Buyers who decide fast and decide often. Product content at scale, channel-native publishing, A/B at the headline + creative + landing-page level, review-mining for objections, paid-creative competitive intel. Cost per output is the metric.

Judgment-buyer audiences

Services · specialty · regulated.

Buyers who hire on perceived expertise and trust signals. Thought-leadership content, authority positioning, regulation/ruling monitoring, review and reputation surface, local-SERP defense where geography matters. Compliance-aware where needed.

Doesn't fit any of these? The audit will tell you. The shapes describe distribution surface, not industry — the same engine runs all three.

§ 07 · Known objections

Answered in advance.

01   what does freddy actually do? ›
Audit your distribution surface, then build and run the AI systems your marketing team would build — content pipelines, publishing, monitoring, competitive intelligence. Engineering work, not campaign work. Engineers + multiple AI sessions per task, humans review every output before it ships, results delivered on a portal you can read.
02   why not just hire an agency? ›
You can. Freddy is one option among many. The bet here is that most marketing work in 2026 is engineering — building and running AI systems with humans reviewing every output — and an engineer-led agency delivers it with less overhead and faster turnaround than a traditional 5–15 person team. If your needs are heavier on brand strategy, live events, or buyer interviews, a traditional agency might fit better.
03   can one person really replace a marketing team? ›
Depends on what the team does. Content research, competitive monitoring, publishing, reporting — yes, those. Buyer interviews, brand strategy, live events — no. The audit tells you which kind of team you have.
04   why so cheap? what's the catch? ›
No catch. No 15 salaries, no rented office, no sales team. You pay for output. Your ceiling is my calendar — which is why I cap at six operate clients.
05   bus factor — what if you disappear? ›
Depends on what you took. Audit-only: you keep the report and the raw data, you take them to whichever agency comes next. Operate as standard agency: I'd arrange continuity at handoff. Operate with an export taken: you have a point-in-time snapshot of the pipelines, runbooks, and keys — you can self-host or hand them to another engineer. Snapshots freeze at handoff date and stop receiving improvements, so most clients let us keep running the live versions; the export is there in case you need clean separation.
06   isn't this just chatgpt with extra steps? ›
ChatGPT is an ingredient. Freddy is the kitchen. Real distribution systems need data ingress, scoring, routing, cost control, audit trails, error handling, secret management, and someone on call when a provider changes their API. A chatbot has none of that.
07   what if the audit finds nothing? ›
If the audit doesn't name at least three specific distribution gaps and a plan to close them, you get a refund. Haven't had to issue one.
08   do I have to continue after the audit? ›
No. The audit is complete on its own. You get the report, the raw data, and full ownership. Operate is a separate decision, made with more information once the audit is in.
