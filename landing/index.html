// objections

The questions you're already asking.

The honest answers. If yours isn't here, email me.

q: why not just hire an agency? ›
Because agencies bill the org chart, not the output. You pay a director you never see, an account manager whose job is to forward emails, and a junior who does the actual work. I cut out everyone except the work. Same deliverables, 3–6× cheaper, faster iteration because nobody has to get approval to change a prompt.
q: can one person really replace a marketing team? ›
Not every marketing team. The ones whose job is mostly content research, competitive monitoring, publishing automation, and reporting — yes, those. The ones doing buyer interviews, brand strategy, and live events — no, you still need humans for that. The audit tells you which kind of team you have.
q: why is this cheaper than an agency? what's the catch? ›
No catch. Not because the work is worse — because there are no 15 salaries and no rented office to cover. You pay for output, not the org chart that produced it. Your ceiling is my calendar, not my margin.
q: what if you get sick / hit by a bus / go on holiday? ›
You own the code. Every system I build for you ships with runbooks, deployment keys in your name, and a README a mid-level engineer can read. If I disappear tomorrow, your distribution keeps running. Try getting that out of an agency contract.
q: how is this different from off-the-shelf AI tools? ›
Off-the-shelf AI tools give you a chatbot with good intentions. A distribution system routes real data through those models, scores the outputs, and publishes the winners. The glue is the whole product. That's what I build. Tools are ingredients; Freddy is the kitchen.
q: what if the audit finds nothing? ›
If the audit doesn't name at least three concrete distribution gaps and give you a prioritized plan, you get a full refund. I haven't had to issue one. I don't expect to.
q: do I have to do build and operate after an audit? ›
No. The audit is a standalone product. You get the report, keep the raw data, and do whatever you want with it. Most clients choose to continue because by then we both know whether there's work worth doing — but the commitment at every phase is explicit and phase-scoped.
