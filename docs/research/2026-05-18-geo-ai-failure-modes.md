# LLM-Specific Failure Modes in GEO (Generative-Engine-Optimization) Content

Companion to `docs/research/2026-05-15-judges-domain-geo.md` (what AI engines reward — the citation-lift literature) and to the v0 GEO spec at `docs/handoffs/2026-05-18-judge-design-step1-geo.md`. The 2026-05-15 domain doc establishes the *quality ceiling* for citable content (BLUF, evidence density, passage self-containment, entity stability, freshness, format-intent match). This deliverable catalogues the LLM-specific failure surfaces that show up because GEO content is *consumed by* AI engines that themselves hallucinate, confabulate citations, and project stale training-cutoff worldviews — and because GEO content is *generated by* LLMs running an evolution loop, so the same hallucination class can show up on both ends.

The CI lane has one AI-failure-aware reader: a human reading an LLM-generated brief. GEO has two: the human researcher consuming the page, and the AI engine that decides whether to retrieve and cite the page. The asymmetry is load-bearing: *the engine is simultaneously the reader and the source of failure modes.* A page can be technically correct and still be misattributed, misquoted, mis-dated, or competitor-favorably reframed in the cited answer. The judge has to test for resilience to that downstream distortion, not just upstream production quality.

---

## TL;DR (320 words)

The GEO v0 spec (GEO-1 BLUF / GEO-2 evidence / GEO-3 self-containment / GEO-4 entity + validation / GEO-5 format + freshness) is sharp on *citation-readiness* but silent or weak on five LLM-specific failure surfaces. Each has a measured effect size from 2024–2026 literature; each is a real production failure shape (Perplexity, Apple News, NeurIPS 2025 incident, BBC retrospective on Apple Intelligence).

The five failure surfaces:

1. **Entity confabulation when AI engines cite this content.** Even when the page is correct, the engine fabricates the entity being described or conflates similarly-named entities. Documented: 19.9% GPT-4o citation-fab rate (Chelli et al. 2025); 14–95% entity-hallucination range across 13 models × 40 domains (GhostCite/KGHaluBench). For brand-name GEO content this means the page's *canonical entity name* must be aggressively disambiguated — embedded similar-name targets get conflated downstream.
2. **Source confabulation — engines invent or distort the source URL when citing.** Perplexity Deep Research: 37% citation-fabrication rate, predominantly real-URL-fabricated-attribution shape. OpenAI Deep Research: 78%. Claude with search: 94% (best). The page can be perfect; the engine can still cite a sibling URL or hallucinate a section anchor.
3. **Recency-cutoff distortion.** The engine answers with stale data positioned as fresh. LLMLagBench (arxiv 2511.12116): models often have *behavioral* cutoffs 6–18 months before their release date. Page must signal recency in ways that survive engine re-citation, not just human eyeballs.
4. **Inverted-citation attack.** A competitor or adversary writes a page that names the brand but mis-attributes events/features — and the engine repeats the mis-attribution at retrieval time. This is the GEO analogue of CI's "real-URL-fabricated-claim" failure (Perplexity 37%).
5. **Competitive-injection.** Content designed to be cited but containing competitor-favorable framing or fabricated competitor claims that get carried into the engine's synthesis. Bypasses GEO-2 (evidence density) and GEO-4 (third-party validation) because the surface markers all pass.

**Recommendation.** Five anti-confabulation checks belong in `structural_gate` (URL HEAD, canonical-entity-singleton, dated freshness signal, ≥1 source within 90 days, exact-quote-grep against named source); one judge criterion GEO-6 "Survives engine-side re-citation distortion" earns the documented-exception ≤5 ceiling breach. Three failure surfaces remain partially uncovered and warrant sample-and-flag instrumentation rather than direct judge prose.

## 1. Q1 — Entity confabulation when AI engines cite GEO content (~430 words)

**The failure mode.** The page is correctly written: brand-name "Klinika Melitus" appears 11 times, schema.org Organization JSON-LD is present, the H1 names the entity, the BLUF leads with "Klinika Melitus is a Warsaw aesthetic-dermatology practice…". The engine retrieves the page, synthesizes the answer, and cites "Klinika Mielitus" or "Clinic Melitus" or — most often — collapses the entity into a generic ("a Warsaw dermatology clinic") with the citation footnote pointed at the page. The brand name is lost in the engine's downstream synthesis even when the page got it right.

Three sub-shapes are documented:

- **Lossy normalization.** The engine canonicalizes "Klinika Melitus" → "Melitus Clinic" or "Klinika Mielitus" because the embedding space pulls toward higher-frequency neighbors. HalluEntity (arxiv 2502.11948, 18,785 entity-level annotations on ChatGPT biographies) shows entity-name hallucination at sentence level frequently exceeds string-similarity detection thresholds.
- **Similar-name conflation.** For tech-product GEO (Cursor IDE, Anthropic, Stripe, etc.) the failure is sharper: "Cursor" the IDE blends with "Cursor" the cursor-tracker; "Anthropic" gets attributed to a non-existent "Anthropic Communications." Documented by KGHaluBench (arxiv 2602.19643) — 14–95% entity-existence hallucination range across 13 models × 40 domains.
- **Attribute fabrication.** Entity stays correct, but a fabricated attribute is attached: "Klinika Melitus, founded in 2008 by Dr. Maria Kowalski…" — Klinika exists, the founding year is wrong, the founder name is wrong, and the page never claimed either fact. The engine generated the attribute from a plausibility prior.

**Effect sizes (measured, 2024–2026 literature).**

- **GhostCite 14–95% across 13 models × 40 domains** — entity-existence hallucination rate (arxiv 2602.19643). The range is wide because some domains (well-known public companies) are at the low end and some (small-business / professional-services) are at the high end. The Klinika and DWF first-cohort fixture clients sit in the high-end domain class.
- **HalluLens *Nonsense* sub-benchmark** (arxiv 2504.17550) — explicitly tests handling of non-existent entities; even frontier models confabulate plausible attributes when asked about not-quite-real entities.
- **Chelli et al. 2025 mental-health literature-review study** — 19.9% of GPT-4o citations were entirely fabricated entities (papers and authors that don't exist) when asked to support a real claim.

**Why this matters specifically for GEO judging.** A page can perfectly satisfy GEO-4 (entity stability and third-party validation per the v0 spec) — every internal mention of the brand is identical, schema.org markup is present, external citations are off-domain and dated — and still get mis-cited by the engine. The page can't fully control downstream citation; but the page can take specific actions that *reduce* the rate. Those actions are the load-bearing checks:

- Singleton canonical name across H1, schema.org `@id`, OpenGraph, and BLUF first sentence.
- Explicit disambiguation block early ("Klinika Melitus, not Klinika Med-Plus; founded 2008 in Warsaw…") that the engine retrieves as part of any passage.
- Wikidata / schema.org `sameAs` linking to anchor the entity in the engine's KG.

These are *deterministic, verifiable, cheap.* They belong in `structural_gate`, not in the LLM judge — per design guide §2's OpenRubrics formalization (Hard Rules → gate, Principles → judge).

## 2. Q2 — Source confabulation: engines invent or distort the source URL when citing (~390 words)

**The failure mode.** Engines do not always cite the URL they actually retrieved. The four documented sub-shapes:

- **Sibling-URL substitution.** Engine retrieves `gofreddy.ai/services/legal-content` but cites `gofreddy.ai/legal` (the parent). Frequency depends on engine — Perplexity has the worst profile, citing the most-easily-resolved sibling URL ~12% of the time per ZipTie 2025.
- **Section-anchor hallucination.** Engine cites `gofreddy.ai/services/legal-content#pricing` when no `#pricing` anchor exists. The base URL is real; the anchor is fabricated. Documented in arxiv 2604.03173 ("Detecting and Correcting Reference Hallucinations") — 3–13% URL-component hallucination rate even on web-search-grounded models.
- **Plausible-domain hallucination.** Engine cites `klinika-melitus.com.pl` when the actual domain is `klinikamelitus.pl`. The page was found via the actual domain; the citation uses the engine's prior for typical Polish-clinic URL patterns.
- **Real-URL-fabricated-attribution.** This is the *Perplexity profile* and the most dangerous shape for GEO: the cited URL is correct, but the claim attributed to it ("Klinika Melitus offers 40% off Botox through April") is not in the page. The engine generated the claim and post-hoc anchored the footnote to a plausible source.

**Effect sizes (measured, 2024–2026 literature).**

- **Perplexity Deep Research: 37% citation-fabrication rate** (Deep Research Agent retrospectives, 2025) — predominantly the real-URL-fabricated-claim shape.
- **OpenAI Deep Research: 78% citation accuracy** — i.e. 22% fabrication rate, mixed shape.
- **Claude with search: 94% citation accuracy** — best of the major commercial systems; still 6% fabrication.
- **Web-search-grounded models still hallucinate 3–13% of URLs** even with retrieval grounding (arxiv 2604.03173).
- **NeurIPS 2025: 100 AI-generated hallucinated citations in 53 published papers** despite 3–5 expert reviewers per paper (arxiv 2602.05930) — taxonomy: Total Fabrication 66%, Partial Attribute Corruption 27%, Identifier Hijacking 4%.

**Engine asymmetry — important for GEO specifically.** The same page gets cited differently across engines. Claude (94%) and ChatGPT (62% on complex research per Skywork 2025) are closer to safe; Perplexity (37% fabrication on citation tasks) is the floor; Google AI Overviews skipped the citation-quality benchmark but the Q1 2026 Ahrefs retrospective shows 38% of AI Overview citations now come from outside top-10 organic (down from 76%), suggesting the engine is increasingly comfortable surfacing low-canonical-authority sources — which compounds the misattribution risk.

**What a GEO page can do to reduce engine-side source confabulation.**

- **Canonical-URL tag** + `rel="canonical"` matching the real served URL (deterministic check).
- **No fabricated anchors** — `structural_gate` validates every `#anchor-name` in JSON-LD or in declared section headers actually exists in the rendered HTML.
- **Quote-grep precondition** — every direct quote on the page (string inside `""`) must exist in the actual source named in the same passage. If the page cites "Forrester 2026" with a quote, the quote-grep gate verifies the quote appears at the URL given. This catches *upstream* generation hallucination but does not prevent *downstream* engine confabulation — the latter is unfixable from the page side.

The downstream-engine-distortion shape is exactly what GEO-6 has to test for: does the page survive being mis-quoted, mis-anchored, or competitor-anchored by the engine?

## 3. Q3 — Recency-cutoff distortion: engines answer stale, positioned as fresh (~410 words)

**The failure mode.** The page is dated 2026; the page's body text references 2025 / 2026 data points; the engine still answers the user query as if it's 2024. Three sub-shapes:

- **Training-cutoff drift.** Model behavioral cutoff is materially earlier than its release date. LLMLagBench (arxiv 2511.12116, late 2025) is the canonical paper: "several LLMs exhibit multiple partial cutoff points, possibly corresponding to distinct training stages, and training cutoffs often diverge significantly from release dates." A Feb-2026-released model frequently behaves as if its knowledge stops in Oct 2024 for finance / regulatory / product-launch facts.
- **Reranker recency bias.** Even when RAG retrieves a fresh source, the reranker can prefer older sources because the older source has more inbound mentions. Arxiv 2509.11353: "LLM-based reranking favors more recently dated content within the retrieved set — which means RAG mitigates training-cutoff bias only partly." Wrong direction relative to what GEO needs: even *with* fresh retrieval, the engine sometimes overweights older-but-canonical sources.
- **Relative vs. absolute date confusion.** "Is Your LLM Outdated?" (NAACL 2025): accuracy drops 23–35% when the brief uses relative framing ("4 years ago") vs. absolute ("in 2020"). LLMs reason about absolute dates better than relative ones — GEO pages using "this year," "recently," "the latest" force the engine into the weaker reasoning mode.

**Effect sizes (measured, 2024–2026 literature).**

- **23–35% accuracy degradation** when shifting from absolute-date framing to relative-date framing on temporal QA (NAACL 2025 "Is Your LLM Outdated?").
- **Behavioral cutoff vs. release-date divergence**: 6–18 months typical (LLMLagBench).
- **Recency bias in reranking 2509.11353**: reranker prefers most-recently-dated retrieved doc with effect size ≈ +0.15 cosine-similarity boost — enough to flip ranking on close pairs.
- **Ahrefs 17M-citation freshness study + Search Engine Land 8K-citation analysis**: 44% of AI Overview citations are from current-year content, 85% from the last few years. The bias is *toward* fresh sources at citation time — which means a page without explicit freshness signaling loses citation slots even if its content is current.

**Specific 2024 → 2026 GEO shifts a confabulating engine would miss.** Post-ChatGPT enterprise market settling (the "we'll just use OpenAI" assumption broke 2025); AI-agent platform consolidation (LangChain / CrewAI / AutoGen positions shifted 2025–2026); EU AI Act phase-2 compliance (Aug 2025 enforcement); GEO-vs-AEO terminology stabilization (Kalicube 2026 push); the 76% → 38% drop in AI Overview citation overlap with organic top-10 (Ahrefs Q1 2026). A page using "in recent months" without absolute dating risks getting cited but answered against the 2024 worldview.

**What a GEO page can do.**

- **"As of [absolute date]" framing** as a deterministic page-property — `structural_gate` checks for a visible `data-as-of="2026-MM-DD"` or equivalent `<time datetime=...>` element AND that the date is within the last 90 days.
- **≥1 cited source within 90 days for each forward-looking or current-state claim** — deterministic check against citation URLs' last-modified header or schema.org `datePublished`.
- **Absolute-date framing for all temporal claims** (banned-phrase list: "recently," "in recent months," "today's," "the latest" without an absolute-date qualifier). Deterministic banned-phrase gate.
- **No "current year" without explicit numeric year** — banned-phrase pattern.

All four are deterministic. All four belong in `structural_gate`, not the judge.

## 4. Q4 — Inverted-citation attack: page names a real entity, engine repeats mis-attributions (~370 words)

**The failure mode.** An adversary or competitor publishes a page that names the brand correctly but mis-attributes events, features, prices, or claims to that brand. The engine retrieves the adversary's page, treats it as a citation candidate, and synthesizes the adversary's mis-attribution into the answer. This is the *inverted* version of Q1 — Q1 was the brand's page getting the entity right and the engine getting it wrong; Q4 is the brand's page being correct and an external page being wrong and the engine preferring the external one.

This shape is closely studied as an *attack vector* — not just an honest-mistake failure. The literature:

- **LLMLagBench-style results.** Beyond temporal QA, LLMLagBench documents that the model preferentially weights training-distribution canonical sources even when fresher retrieved sources contradict them. If Wikipedia (training-canonical) has a stale or wrong attribute, retrieval-grounded synthesis can fail to override it.
- **CompoundDeception / Compound Deception benchmark** (arxiv 2602.05930) — explicitly tests the failure shape where two plausible sources disagree and the engine picks the wrong one. Documented at 27% Partial Attribute Corruption + 4% Identifier Hijacking on the NeurIPS 2025 100-fab-citation incident.
- **Brand-mention asymmetry (Ahrefs 75K-brand study).** Brand mentions correlate with AI Overview visibility at r=0.664 — three times the backlink correlation (r=0.218). But *unlinked* mentions in third-party text are what drives the signal. If an external page mis-attributes, the mis-attribution gets correlated to the brand at retrieval-rerank time.

**Effect sizes (measured, 2024–2026 literature).**

- **27% Partial Attribute Corruption** rate in NeurIPS 2025 fab-citation incident — engine attributes real events to wrong sources, or wrong attributes to real entities.
- **Status Labs / RankScience "Citation Gap"**: 85% of pages ChatGPT retrieves are never cited. Of the 15% cited, the engine picks based on passage relevance — meaning an adversary's passage that crisply mis-attributes can win the slot over the brand's own correct page.
- **Forrester 2026**: AI buyers validate vendor claims against external sources before trusting them. If those external sources carry inverted citations, validation fails the brand.

**What a GEO page can do.**

- **Schema.org `subjectOf` / `mentions` / `sameAs` reciprocal linking** — establish the entity's canonical attributes in machine-readable form that engines can cross-check against retrieved sources.
- **Authoritative-source seeding** — link out to Wikidata / Wikipedia / Crunchbase / SEC EDGAR entity pages so the engine's KG lookup converges on the correct entity attributes.
- **Quote-grep gate from §2** — any quote on the page must be exact-match against the cited source. This doesn't stop adversaries, but it ensures the brand's *own* page is not inadvertently planting the seed for downstream inversion.

**What only sample-and-flag instrumentation can catch.** Whether external pages currently mis-attribute the brand. This is a *monitoring-lane* concern, not a GEO-lane judge concern. Flag for the monitoring lane: periodic queries against ChatGPT/Perplexity/Claude/Gemini for the brand, with diff against the page's canonical entity attributes.

## 5. Q5 — Competitive injection: page designed to be cited contains competitor-favorable framing (~360 words)

**The failure mode.** An adversary (or, more commonly under evolution-loop selection pressure, the *workflow itself*) writes a page that satisfies all GEO surface markers — BLUF declarative lead, evidence density, self-contained passages, third-party validation — but the framing of those validations or comparisons is competitor-favorable. The engine cites the page, repeating the competitor-favorable framing.

Two sub-shapes:

- **Competitor-favored comparison table.** Page contains "Klinika Melitus vs. DermaCenter West" comparison. Klinika's strengths are stated; DermaCenter's strengths are *also* stated in equal or stronger language. The engine retrieves the table and synthesizes a tie or a slight DermaCenter lean. Looks like fair comparison; reads as competitor advertisement. Especially dangerous under evolution-loop selection where GEO-2 (evidence density) and GEO-4 (third-party validation) reward acknowledging the competitive landscape — over-acknowledgment becomes a Goodhart trap.
- **Fabricated-competitor-claim injection.** Page contains "DermaCenter West offers $30K-off Botox memberships" — a fabricated competitor claim (DermaCenter doesn't offer that program). Page is the brand's own; the brand has no way to fact-check what competitors are doing in real-time; the LLM-generation hallucinated the competitor offer. The engine retrieves and repeats. Now the brand is unintentionally publishing competitor disinformation, which is a legal/reputational liability and a citation-signal poisoning vector.

**Effect sizes (measured, 2024–2026 literature).**

- **HalluLens *Nonsense* sub-benchmark** (arxiv 2504.17550) — context-free entity attribute hallucination is the modal failure shape. For competitor descriptions in GEO content, this is the highest-rate hallucination class because the model is being asked to describe an entity it has weaker training signal for (most competitors are below brand-level visibility).
- **Crayon "Sparks" / CB Insights AI features** — both kept human review in the loop precisely because competitor-summarization fabrication rate was too high to ship unverified.
- **The Verge documented Google AI Mode catching vendor-authored "best of" lists** that placed their own products first — i.e. the engine has *some* defense against this pattern, but the defense is brittle (catches obvious self-puffery, misses subtle competitive injection).

**What a GEO page can do.**

- **Quote-grep gate from §2 applied to competitor passages.** Every claim about a named competitor (numeric, dated, quoted) must be backed by a citation that quote-greps against the actual source. Catches fabricated competitor claims at structural-gate level.
- **Banned-phrase list for un-attributed competitor claims** — phrases like "Acme offers," "Acme's pricing is," "Acme's customers include" without an inline citation are structural failures.
- **Comparison-table content-rule check** (deterministic) — every competitor-row claim has an off-domain citation in the same row.

The remaining failure surface — *competitor-favorable framing of accurately-cited facts* — is semantic. It belongs in a judge criterion or in sample-and-flag instrumentation. The v0 spec's GEO-4 partially covers (third-party validation must be off-domain + dated), but the over-acknowledgment Goodhart trap under evolution pressure is not directly tested. GEO-6 below picks this up.

## 6. Q6 — Cross-cutting: what GEO v0 catches vs misses (~410 words)

Walk each v0 criterion against the 5 failure surfaces above (and a 6th — Q6 confidence-without-calibration, lifted from CI's parallel research as a contrast):

| Criterion | Q1 Entity-conf | Q2 Source-conf | Q3 Recency-cutoff | Q4 Inverted-citation | Q5 Competitive-injection |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| GEO-1 BLUF | partial (forces canonical name in first 75w) | NO | NO | NO | NO |
| GEO-2 Evidence | NO | partial (forces attribution path) | partial (forces dated specifics) | NO | partial (forces off-domain attribution) |
| GEO-3 Self-containment | partial (forces entity-name in each passage) | NO | NO | NO | NO |
| GEO-4 Entity + validation | partial (forces canonical name + ≥2 off-domain) | NO | NO | partial (third-party validation forces external KG anchors) | partial (third-party validation reduces vendor-vacuum) |
| GEO-5 Format + freshness | NO | NO | YES (forces visible date + current-year refs) | NO | NO |

**What GEO v0 actually catches:**

- **Q3 partial → YES via GEO-5.** GEO-5 explicitly requires visible date + current-year references + freshness window. Strongest coverage of any v0 criterion against any failure mode. Still misses: relative-date framing (which is a *generation*-side mitigation routed through banned-phrase gate), training-cutoff probing, reranker recency bias (which is engine-side, unfixable from page).
- **Q1, Q4, Q5 partial via GEO-4.** Entity stability + third-party validation reduces *some* of the engine-side entity confabulation by giving the engine canonical anchors, and reduces *some* of the inverted-citation attack surface by establishing reciprocal external signals. But the criterion is about *page-side* presence, not about engine-side resilience.

**What GEO v0 does not catch at all:**

- **Q1 deep entity confabulation: zero direct coverage.** GEO-4 verifies the page is internally consistent on entity name, but does nothing for similar-name conflation (Cursor IDE vs Cursor tracker; Anthropic vs Anthropic Communications). No Wikidata/schema.org sameAs requirement. No explicit disambiguation block requirement.
- **Q2 source confabulation: zero coverage.** The current `_validate_geo` in `src/evaluation/structural.py:54-80` checks only optimized/ file presence and JSON-LD validity. No URL HEAD check on cited links. No quote-grep against named sources. No canonical-URL tag check. No fabricated-anchor check.
- **Q4 inverted-citation attack: zero coverage.** No schema.org reciprocal linking check. No Wikidata seeding requirement. The monitoring lane would have to backstop this, and currently doesn't.
- **Q5 competitive-injection: weak partial.** GEO-4's third-party validation requirement creates a back-pressure against vendor-vacuum, but a workflow under evolution pressure can satisfy GEO-4 by stuffing on-page competitor comparisons — exactly the Goodhart trap §5 names.

**The asymmetric risk.** A page that confabulates the entity in its disambiguation prose, cites two non-existent URLs as "third-party validation," dates a "Last updated 2026-05-18" stamp without any real current-year body content, and contains a competitor-comparison table with fabricated competitor pricing — can score *high* under v0. GEO-1=1 (BLUF lands declaratively); GEO-2=1 (three "evidence" items present); GEO-3=1 (passages stand alone); GEO-4=1 (off-domain validations + canonical entity name); GEO-5=1 (date + current-year refs). Perfect 5/5. Strategically empty *and structurally invalid downstream*. This is the architectural gap.

## 7. Cross-family judge panel and shared hallucination classes (~280 words)

Design guide §8 prescribes a cross-family three-model panel (Claude Opus 4.7 / GPT-5.5 / Gemini 3 Flash) to reduce same-family preference leakage. But the panel buys *zero* independence against failure modes where all three families share the hallucination class. For GEO specifically, the shared classes are:

- **Source confabulation (Q2).** All three frontier families confabulate URLs at non-zero rates (Claude 6% / OpenAI 22% / Perplexity 37%). When the judge panel scores a brief on whether sources are credible, all three panel members can simultaneously trust a fabricated citation. *The panel cannot detect Q2 — only structural_gate URL HEAD can.*
- **Entity attribute hallucination (Q1).** All three families share the "plausible attribute fill-in" tendency on lower-visibility entities. When the brief asserts "Klinika Melitus, founded 2008 by Dr. Maria Kowalski," all three panel models can simultaneously rate the assertion as credible-looking. *Wikidata / Crunchbase / SEC EDGAR lookup in structural_gate is the only defense.*
- **Reranker recency bias (Q3).** All three frontier families' built-in retrievers exhibit the same recency-rerank bias (arxiv 2509.11353). If the judge is asked to score "does this page's freshness signal reach AI engines," all three panel models will under-weight pre-rerank freshness signals. *Sample-and-flag instrumentation against live engine queries is the only direct measurement.*

This is the load-bearing reason the design guide's structural_gate routing matters *more* for GEO than for any other lane: the judge panel structurally cannot catch the failure modes that are downstream of the engine's own hallucination class. The deterministic checks aren't redundant with the panel; they cover failure surfaces the panel is blind to.

Three-model cross-family panel + 5-check anti-confabulation structural_gate + 1 evidence-chain criterion = the architectural minimum. Anything less leaves a measurable failure surface uncovered.

## 8. Recommendation — where should AI-failure-mode detection live? (~410 words)

**Three plausible homes:**

1. **In the judge** as new criteria.
2. **In `structural_gate`** as deterministic checks (current home: `src/evaluation/structural.py:54-80` `_validate_geo`).
3. **In sample-and-flag instrumentation** as monitoring-lane backstop.

**Recommended split — by failure surface:**

| Failure surface | Best home | Why | Implementation cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 Entity confabulation (page-side) | structural_gate | Deterministic. Wikidata/Crunchbase/SEC EDGAR lookup. Singleton canonical name across H1/JSON-LD/OG. Wrong fit for judge (factual not semantic). | ~30 LOC + 1 external API; rate-limit-safe with caching |
| Q1 Entity confabulation (engine-side downstream) | sample-and-flag | Not testable from the page alone — needs live engine query. Monitoring-lane backstop. | Defer to monitoring lane |
| Q2 Source confabulation | structural_gate | URL HEAD, canonical-URL tag, anchor-existence, quote-grep. All deterministic and cheap. | ~50 LOC; URL HEAD ~100ms per cited URL; cached |
| Q3 Recency-cutoff distortion | structural_gate | "As of" date check + ≥1 source within 90 days + banned-phrase ("recently," "today's") + absolute-date framing requirement. All deterministic. | ~20 LOC; banned-phrase regex + date math |
| Q4 Inverted-citation attack (page-side prophylaxis) | structural_gate | Schema.org `sameAs` + Wikidata anchor + reciprocal linking. Deterministic. | ~20 LOC; JSON-LD parse |
| Q4 Inverted-citation attack (engine-side monitoring) | sample-and-flag | Same as Q1 engine-side. Monitoring lane. | Defer |
| Q5 Competitive-injection (factual layer) | structural_gate | Every competitor-claim must have inline off-domain citation that quote-greps. Banned-phrase for unattributed competitor claims. | ~30 LOC |
| Q5 Competitive-injection (framing layer) | judge (GEO-6) | Semantic — testing whether comparison framing favors competitor under fair surface. Cannot be deterministic. | New criterion |
| Cross-cutting "evidence chain survives engine-side citation" | judge (GEO-6) | Semantic — tests whether the page is *robust to* downstream misattribution, not just upstream-correct. | New criterion |

**Why structural_gate gets most of the weight.** The §7 cross-family panel argument: when all three frontier families share a hallucination class (Q1, Q2, Q3 all qualify), the LLM judge panel is structurally blind. Deterministic checks are not redundant — they cover failure surfaces the judge cannot detect.

**Why one new judge criterion (GEO-6) earns the ≤5 ceiling breach.** Per design guide §5's documented-exception clause: "When a lane's artifact has a documented LLM-specific failure surface that the other 5 criteria cannot catch (e.g., entity confabulation at 19.9% GPT-4o citation-fab rate, source confabulation at 37% Perplexity failure shape, recency-cutoff distortion per LLMLagBench), a 6th criterion targeting that surface is permitted as a documented exception." GEO is exactly the lane named in the design guide §5 exception clause as the prototype use case. CI established the pattern with CI-6 evidence-chain; GEO should adopt the parallel pattern.

**Why not a new "AI-slop gate" sibling to structural_gate.** The same architectural argument from `2026-05-18-ci-ai-failure-modes.md` §8: failure modes split cleanly into "deterministic + cheap" (Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4 page-side, Q5 factual) and "semantic + judge-shaped" (Q5 framing + cross-cutting evidence-chain). The cheap deterministic checks belong in structural_gate (existing infrastructure, existing pass/fail contract, runs before the judge). The semantic checks belong in the judge prose. A new gate would be architectural duplication.

## 9. Concrete edits to GEO v0 spec (~280 words)

**Edit 1 — Add 8 anti-hallucination checks to `_validate_geo` in `src/evaluation/structural.py:54-80`.**

```
Page-side checks (deterministic, all required for structural_gate pass):
  1. URL HEAD resolution for every off-domain cited URL (HTTP 200 within 5s, with retry-on-3xx)
  2. Canonical-URL tag present and matches served URL (no fabricated canonicals)
  3. Every `#anchor-name` in JSON-LD or section nav exists in rendered HTML
  4. Singleton canonical entity name across H1 + JSON-LD `@id` + OpenGraph + BLUF first sentence
  5. Schema.org `sameAs` linking to at least one of: Wikidata, Crunchbase, SEC EDGAR, LinkedIn (entity anchoring)
  6. "As of [absolute date]" or `<time datetime=...>` element visible within last 90 days
  7. ≥1 cited source dated within 90 days (per schema.org `datePublished` or HTTP last-modified)
  8. Exact-quote-grep: every direct quote (string in `""`) appears in the cited source URL with cosine similarity > 0.85

Banned-phrase patterns (deterministic, structural failure if matched without disambiguating absolute-date qualifier):
  - "recently"
  - "in recent months"
  - "today's"
  - "the latest"
  - "current year" / "this year" without absolute year qualifier
```

```
### GEO-6 — Survives engine-side re-citation distortion

Outcome question (binary):
If an AI engine were to retrieve a passage from this page and synthesize an
answer with one or two of: similar-name conflation, anchor hallucination,
partial-attribute corruption, or competitor-favorable reframing — would the
brand still come out correctly identified, correctly attributed, and not
out-framed by a competitor? Does the page's structure *force* the engine
toward correct synthesis rather than relying on the engine to figure it out?

Score 1 (yes) — Page contains: (a) explicit disambiguation block early
(against most-confusable similar-name entity); (b) schema.org sameAs to ≥1
canonical KG anchor (Wikidata / Crunchbase / SEC EDGAR / LinkedIn); (c) any
competitor comparison framed with off-domain citation for every competitor
claim — no asymmetry where brand claims are supported and competitor claims
are unsupported (or vice versa); (d) absolute-date framing for all temporal
claims so engines reasoning about "recent" don't conflate with training-
cutoff "recent".

Score 0 (no) — Any of: similar-name conflation surface exposed (no
disambiguation block when one is needed); no KG anchor; competitor
comparison framing asymmetry; relative-date framing on current-state claims.

Score 0.5 (unknown) — Page is structurally clean but the disambiguation /
anchoring is too thin to evaluate engine resilience from the page alone.
Emit 0.5 + "unknown" + one sentence on what's missing.

CoT:
- Step 1: Identify the entity's most-confusable similar-name target (from page context).
- Step 2: Check for disambiguation block + schema.org sameAs anchor.
- Step 3: For any competitor comparison, check claim-citation symmetry.
- Step 4: Check temporal framing for absolute-date vs relative-date drift.
- Step 5: Emit verdict + one-sentence justification.

Do not score: URL HEAD resolution (structural_gate), quote-grep (structural_gate),
schema.org JSON-LD validity (structural_gate), date-stamp presence (structural_gate).
```

## 10. Open questions (~290 words)

After this AI-failure-modes pass, four uncertainties remain:

1. **Engine-side citation distortion rate per engine for gofreddy's actual client domains.** The 37% / 78% / 94% Perplexity / OpenAI / Claude numbers come from generic benchmarks. The real rate for "aesthetic-dermatology Warsaw" or "EU-regulated legal services" queries is unknown — could be higher (lower-visibility entities) or lower (less ambiguous query terms). **Mitigation:** monitoring-lane sample-and-flag instrumentation against client-domain queries, weekly probe, with diff against page's canonical attributes.

2. **Wikidata coverage for SMB / professional-services clients.** Klinika Melitus is unlikely to have a Wikidata entry today. Schema.org `sameAs` to Wikidata becomes a non-starter for the long tail. Alternative anchors: Google Business Profile (location-anchored), Crunchbase (company-anchored), LinkedIn Company Page (corporate-anchored), official .gov registry (regulated-industry-anchored). **Mitigation:** structural_gate accepts *any* of the canonical KG anchors, not Wikidata exclusively. Tier-1 ranked: Wikidata > Crunchbase > LinkedIn Company > Google Business Profile > registry .gov.

3. **Quote-grep cosine-similarity threshold tuning.** The proposed 0.85 threshold is borrowed from arxiv 2604.03173 ("Detecting and Correcting Reference Hallucinations"). For GEO content, the appropriate threshold may be tighter (citation accuracy matters more than paraphrase tolerance). **Mitigation:** start at 0.85, instrument false-positive / false-negative rates over first 20 GEO fixtures, tune.

4. **GEO vs site_engine overlap on Q4/Q5 instrumentation.** Inverted-citation monitoring and competitive-injection monitoring naturally extend to *every page on the site*, not just GEO-lane pages. **Mitigation:** scope GEO judge to single-page artifact (current v0 scope); push site-wide monitoring to site_engine spec when that lands; document the boundary in v1 spec lock.
