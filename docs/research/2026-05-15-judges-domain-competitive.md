# Domain Research: What Makes an Excellent Competitive-Intelligence Brief

**Date:** 2026-05-15
**Purpose:** Ground the `competitive` lane judge in published competitive-intelligence frameworks, named consulting-firm exemplars, and practitioner heuristics — not statistical properties or transferred meta-patterns.
**Scope:** Synthesis of 7 frameworks (Helmer 7 Powers, Porter Five Forces, Roger Martin Playing to Win, April Dunford positioning, Andy Raskin strategic narrative, Christensen jobs-to-be-done/disruption, Andy Grove strategic inflection point), 4 practitioner sources (SCIP, Klue, Octopus Intelligence, Competitive Intelligence Alliance), 2 consulting-firm methodologies (McKinsey war games, CB Insights strategy teardowns), and 1 analytic-tradecraft tradition (Heuer & Pherson Structured Analytic Techniques).

---

## 1. What Makes a Competitive Brief Actionable

The strongest convergence across consulting firms, practitioner playbooks, and SCIP-aligned methodology is a single distinction: **a brief that changes how a decision-maker thinks or acts is intelligence; a brief that catalogues competitor activity is a data dump.** Octopus Intelligence states this directly: "If it doesn't change how your company thinks and acts, you've just created a data dump, not intelligence." This is the foundational quality dimension — every other dimension flows from it.

### Empirical patterns from real briefs that produced decisions

**Pattern 1 — Point of view at the top, evidence below.** Octopus Intelligence: "Your point of view should sit prominently at the very top of your document, captured succinctly in your headline rather than buried under heaps of data or preamble." Klue's executive-briefing template, taught to thousands of B2B CI practitioners, hardcodes this as a five-element structure: **Headline → Rationale → Comparison → Implications → Recommendations.** The headline is a claim, not a topic.

**Pattern 2 — Every observation forces a "So what?"** The Competitive Intelligence Alliance frames this as the discipline that distinguishes intelligence from reporting: "every observation should be followed by an inference and a 'so what' that connects to a specific business or marketing decision." It is not enough to note that a competitor launched a feature; the brief must state what that launch implies for *us* and what *we* must decide in response. McKinsey's war-gaming practice operationalises the same idea — the output of a war game is "strategic guidance on the industry's direction, the most promising types of moves, the company's competitive strengths and weaknesses, and where to focus further analysis," never a tactical playbook.

**Pattern 3 — Top 3–5 findings, ruthlessly prioritised.** Klue, the Competitive Intelligence Alliance, and Octopus all converge on five-to-seven findings per cycle, with three to five recommendations. The Alliance frames executive failure modes explicitly: "time constraints," "information overload," "poor delivery channels." Brevity is not a stylistic preference; it is a structural recognition that decision-makers will not consume the eighth finding.

**Pattern 4 — Trajectory beats snapshot.** CB Insights' strategy teardowns (Apple, Google, Amazon) impose a standardised three-part structure on every priority area: **"WHAT it's DOING now" → "WHERE it's GOING next" → "WHY is this a priority?"** The "where it's going" frame is built from convergent signals across patents, M&A, earnings transcripts, headcount, and product launches — what the teardown explicitly calls "clues" to future direction. Andy Grove's *Only the Paranoid Survive* names the same skill: spotting a strategic inflection point, defined as "a time in the life of a business when its fundamentals are about to change," requires reading the trajectory of 10X forces (competition, technology, customers, suppliers, complementors, regulation), not the current snapshot.

**Pattern 5 — Recommendations are time-bound and specific.** Product Marketing Alliance: actionable insights must be "specific and measurable" — they reject "vague statements like 'increase productivity'" and require concrete commitments like "investing in a new platform." The Competitive Intelligence Alliance adds that recommendations must be "specific, actionable, and tied to business impact."

### Dimensions practitioners cite

Synthesising across the practitioner sources, the dimensions CI directors genuinely evaluate are:
1. **Has a point of view** (vs. neutral cataloguing)
2. **Connects evidence → inference → implication → recommendation** at every step
3. **Frames trajectory, not just snapshot**
4. **Surfaces a hard prioritisation** (3–5 findings, 2–3 recommended actions)
5. **Names the consequence of inaction**
6. **Specifies time-bound, concrete actions**
7. **Degrades gracefully when evidence is partial** — the SCIP-aligned discipline is "well-informed guesses" with uncertainty flagged, never silent extrapolation

## 2. What Separates Great Competitive Intel from Mediocre

Named practitioners identify specific, recurring failure modes — not abstract criticisms.

**Klue's seven named mistakes** (from their CI-framework problems playbook) — these are the most concretely named failure modes in the practitioner literature:
1. **Poor competitive differentiation** — "Generic facts like 'Competitor X has a strong brand' don't help teams differentiate." The fix: start with the unique value proposition, then weave in supporting evidence.
2. **Decentralised intelligence** — "There are more variations of battlecards floating around Google docs than Taylor Swift break-up songs." The fix: one source of truth.
3. **Inaccurate internal messaging** — company optimism uncorrected by competitive reality.
4. **No strategic direction** — collection without an alignment to business objectives.
5. **Late delivery** — "Nearly half [of CI consumers] said their reps don't know who they're competing with until the negotiation stage."
6. **Unreliable CRM data** — can't prioritise competitors without clean data.
7. **Reactive posture** — perpetually responding instead of pre-empting.

**Contify's named myths** add a complementary set of failure modes:
- Treating data, information, and intelligence as interchangeable. The hierarchy is real: "Intelligence is information that provides an insight."
- Expecting CI to reveal competitor plans rather than infer strategic direction from signals.
- Over-relying on AI: "AI only helps in the efficient processing of information," not analytic judgement.

**Heuer & Pherson** (the Structured Analytic Techniques tradition the U.S. intelligence community uses) name the cognitive failures CI inherits when transplanted into business contexts:
- **Confirmation bias** — building the analysis to fit a prior. The countermeasure is Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH): identify mutually exclusive hypotheses, evaluate evidence against each, and reject hypotheses that accumulate too much inconsistent evidence.
- **Unexamined assumptions** — Key Assumptions Check forces the analyst to list the premises the analysis rests on.
- **Tunnel vision** — What If? analysis stress-tests against alternative futures.

**McKinsey's war-gaming literature** names the same failure mode practitioners hit when they only look at current rivals: "competitor neglect." McKinsey's specific countermeasure is multi-round simulation where senior leaders and industry experts build "deeply researched profiles" of two competitors, then project likely actions and reactions. The brief that emerges names competitor *response patterns*, not competitor *positions*.

**The "kill your darlings" discipline** — adapted from editing — is named by multiple CI commentators as the single most useful skill transfer for a category that produces too much content and too little of consequence. The Competitive Intelligence Alliance's phrasing of the executive risk is that "executives tend to be surrounded by human filters whose job seems to be to keep those executives isolated from reality" — so the CI brief is one of the rare channels that *should* carry uncomfortable truths upward, and a brief that has been edited to comfort the executive has failed.

## 3. Industry Frameworks the Rubric Should Leverage

These are the frameworks competitive-intelligence practitioners and corporate strategists actually use. Each carries a specific quality test that a brief should pass.

### Hamilton Helmer — 7 Powers
Helmer's contribution is the **two-part test for sustainable competitive advantage**: every power requires a **Benefit** (materially augmented cash flow through price, cost, or reduced investment) AND a **Barrier** ("an obstacle that makes copying it unattractive"). The seven powers are: Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, Process Power. The "3 S's" unifying principle: Power exists when an attribute is simultaneously **Superior, Significant, and Sustainable**.

The diagnostic for a brief: **does it identify which power (if any) the competitor has or is building, and does it apply both halves of the test (Benefit AND Barrier)?** A brief that claims a competitor has scale economies without naming the cost-curve mechanism, or claims network effects without identifying winner-take-all dynamics, fails the test.

Counter-positioning is the Helmer power most frequently mishandled. Helmer's specific recognition pattern is the **Five Stages** an incumbent moves through when a challenger uses counter-positioning: Denial → Ridicule → Fear → Anger → Capitulation. A brief on a disruption pattern should locate the incumbent on this curve.

### Michael Porter — Five Forces + "What Is Strategy?"
Porter's central argument is that strategists "define competition too narrowly, as if it occurred only among today's direct competitors." Industry profitability is shaped by five forces, not one: rivalry among existing competitors, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitutes, and threat of new entrants. A competitive brief that only analyses direct rivals has, by Porter's own definition, missed four-fifths of the structure.

The "What Is Strategy?" essay adds the **operational effectiveness vs. strategic positioning** distinction: doing the same things better than rivals is not strategy; doing different things, or doing things differently, is. The diagnostic for a brief: **does it distinguish whether the competitor's advantage is operational-effectiveness (replicable) or strategic-positioning (sustainable trade-offs)?**

### Roger Martin — Playing to Win
Martin & Lafley's Strategy Choice Cascade — Winning Aspiration → Where to Play → How to Win → Capabilities → Management Systems — gives the brief a specific quality test: **the where-to-play and how-to-win choices are inseparable.** Martin's strongest extension of Helmer's framework is the explicit "Can't/Won't Test": sustainable advantage requires a barrier that competitors can't or won't replicate. A brief that names a competitor's "how to win" without naming the corresponding "where to play" — or vice versa — has not yet earned the strategic claim.

### April Dunford — Obviously Awesome / Sales Pitch
Dunford's distinctive insight is that competitive analysis should **start with competitive alternatives, not problems or visions**. Her positioning framework has five (plus one) components: competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value → target market → market category. Critically, competitive alternatives include non-competitive options — what customers would do if your product didn't exist, including "hire an assistant" or "do nothing." A brief that only analyses named competitors and ignores "do nothing" or substitutes has failed the Dunford test.

### Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative
Raskin's five-element structure (Big Relevant Change → Winners and Losers → Promised Land → Magic Gifts → Evidence) is a competitive-positioning frame, not just a sales-deck frame. The distinctive contribution for CI: **"Name the Enemy" — but the enemy is an old mindset, not a competitor.** Raskin: "your buyer's enemy is not competitors. It's not even a problem the buyer faces." The brief tests against this by asking whether the strategic narrative the *competitor* is telling targets a mindset shift (powerful) or attacks rivals (weak). Zuora's "subscription economy" narrative is Raskin's canonical example — it didn't attack Salesforce; it named the shift from ownership to subscription as the world-change.

### Clayton Christensen — Disruptive Innovation + Jobs-to-be-Done
Christensen's contribution is the **low-end disruption pattern** — a challenger enters with a "good enough" offering at the bottom of a market segment incumbents have abandoned because their economics favour higher-margin customers. Jobs-to-be-Done reframes competitive analysis around customer "progress they're trying to make" rather than product categories. The diagnostic for a brief: **does it analyse the customer job and the segment economics, or only the product feature comparison?** A brief that misses a low-end disruptor because the disruptor is "worse" by incumbent feature criteria has fallen into exactly the trap Christensen named.

### Andy Grove — Strategic Inflection Point
Grove's 10X forces (competition, technology, customers, suppliers, complementors, regulation) give a concrete checklist for the "trajectory" dimension. The strategic inflection point is the moment fundamentals are about to change, and the test for a brief is whether it identifies which 10X forces are currently 10X — most are not. Grove's prescription is decisive action under uncertainty: "act even when outcomes are not entirely clear. Waiting too long to respond can mean losing ground."

### BCG Advantage Matrix
BCG's matrix gives the **industry-context test**: classify the industry as Volume (few approaches, big advantage size), Stalemate (few approaches, small size), Fragmented (many approaches, small size), or Specialisation (many approaches, big size). A recommendation tuned to a Volume industry is wrong for a Stalemate one. A brief that makes a strategic recommendation without locating the industry on this matrix is recommending in the abstract.

### Heuer & Pherson — Structured Analytic Techniques (tradecraft)
ACH, Key Assumptions Check, and What If? analysis aren't strategy frameworks — they're hygiene. The diagnostic: **does the brief consider alternative hypotheses, surface its assumptions, and stress-test its conclusions against a plausible alternative future?**

## 4. Proposed Judge Criteria (5–7) Grounded in This Research

Each criterion below names the framework it's grounded in, the failure mode it catches, and what a low/high score looks like.

### CI-A — Has a Point of View, Not a Catalogue
**What it evaluates:** Does the brief lead with a strategic claim (headline-as-claim) or with description (headline-as-topic)?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** Octopus Intelligence and Klue both anchor their executive-briefing templates on point-of-view-first structure. The Competitive Intelligence Alliance names "no point of view" as the most common failure mode in CI newsletters. This is the dimension most CI directors test for first.
**High-scoring example pattern:** "Acme's bundling strategy targets our SMB renewal base; if we don't respond by Q3, we lose the discount-sensitive 30% of the cohort." (Claim first, evidence supports.)
**Low-scoring failure mode:** "Acme launched a bundle on May 1. The bundle includes X, Y, Z. Pricing is $99." (Catalogue, no claim.)

### CI-B — Evidence → Inference → Implication → Recommendation Chain
**What it evaluates:** For each finding, does the brief explicitly walk from observation, to what it lets us infer, to what it implies for us, to what we should do? Does it force a "So what?" at every stage?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** This is the most-cited operational definition of "actionable" across SCIP-aligned methodology, Octopus Intelligence, the Competitive Intelligence Alliance, and Klue. The Heuer & Pherson tradecraft demands the same chain — the analyst must show their reasoning.
**High-scoring failure mode caught:** A brief that ends with implications but no recommendations, or jumps from evidence directly to recommendations without showing the inferential step.
**Low-scoring failure mode:** "Competitor X is doubling down on AI." (No so-what. The Octopus rule applies: add the so-what or cut the line.)

### CI-C — Trajectory, Not Just Snapshot
**What it evaluates:** Does the brief reason about where the competitor is heading (next 6–18 months) using convergent signals — patents, M&A, headcount, earnings-call language, product roadmap — not just where they are today?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** CB Insights' strategy teardowns enforce the WHAT-NOW / WHERE-NEXT / WHY-PRIORITY structure on every section; this is the model practitioners cite. Andy Grove's 10X forces give the trajectory checklist. The Wayne Gretzky aphorism ("skate to where the puck is going") is the most-cited CI mantra for a reason.
**Failure modes:** (a) Only describing current state; (b) Asserting trajectory without naming the convergent signals; (c) Linear extrapolation from one signal.

### CI-D — Identifies the Mechanism of Advantage (Helmer / Porter Test)
**What it evaluates:** When the brief claims a competitor has an advantage, does it identify which of Helmer's 7 Powers (or which Porter force) underlies it, AND apply the Benefit-and-Barrier test? Does it distinguish operational effectiveness from strategic positioning?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** Helmer's two-part test is the most rigorous published frame for separating real moats from claimed ones; Porter's operational-effectiveness/strategic-positioning distinction is the most-cited tool for separating replicable advantages from sustainable ones. Roger Martin's analysis of Helmer endorses this as the "Can't/Won't Test."
**Failure modes:** (a) Asserting an advantage without identifying its mechanism; (b) Naming a mechanism (e.g., "network effects") without showing winner-take-all dynamics or the barrier; (c) Confusing operational effectiveness ("they ship faster") with strategic positioning ("they made trade-offs we can't").

### CI-E — Names the Strategic Posture and the Hard Trade-Off
**What it evaluates:** Does the brief recommend a specific strategic posture (attack / defend / flank / cooperate / ignore) and surface the trade-off the recommendation requires us to accept? Does it pass Roger Martin's "where to play / how to win" inseparability test?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** Roger Martin's Playing to Win frame is the dominant strategic-choice framework taught in B-schools and corp-dev programs. The offensive/defensive/cooperative options taxonomy is standard in strategic-posture literature. The hard trade-off is the test of whether a recommendation is real strategy or a wish list.
**Failure modes:** (a) Multiple recommendations with no priority; (b) Recommendations that require no trade-off (red flag — real strategy always costs something); (c) Recommendations that name "how to win" without "where to play."

### CI-F — Surfaces Uncomfortable Truths and Considers Alternative Hypotheses
**What it evaluates:** Does the brief surface evidence that contradicts the favoured narrative or the executive's prior? Does it consider at least one plausible alternative hypothesis (ACH-style) for the competitor's behaviour? Does it name its key assumptions explicitly?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** Heuer & Pherson's Structured Analytic Techniques are the published standard for hypothesis discipline in intelligence analysis. The Competitive Intelligence Alliance names "bias — leaders may dismiss insights contradicting their market assumptions" as a top-five failure mode. The "kill your darlings" discipline is widely cited as what separates CI that lands from CI that placates.
**Failure modes:** (a) Single-hypothesis analysis; (b) No surfaced assumptions; (c) Conclusions that conveniently confirm an internal narrative without disconfirming evidence engaged.

### CI-G — Hard Prioritisation: Top 2–3 Actions, Time-Bound
**What it evaluates:** Does the brief end with a small number (ideally 2–3) of time-bound, specific recommendations, with the rest cut or explicitly deprioritised? Are recommendations concrete enough to commit to ("invest in X by Q3") rather than vague ("improve productivity")?
**Why this is what experts evaluate:** Klue's "top 3–5 findings" rule, the Competitive Intelligence Alliance's "specific, actionable, tied to business impact" standard, and Product Marketing Alliance's rejection of "vague statements like 'increase productivity'" all converge here. The executive failure mode is "information overload" — the discipline is editing.
**Failure modes:** (a) 8+ recommendations of equal weight; (b) Recommendations with no deadline; (c) Vague verbs ("explore," "consider," "evaluate") rather than commitments.

## Notes for Rubric Authors

1. **CI-A through CI-G map cleanly to the existing CI-1 through CI-8 surface area but reground them.** Specifically: CI-A subsumes "central thesis"; CI-B subsumes "reasoning chain proportionality"; CI-C subsumes "competitor trajectory"; CI-D is new (mechanism of advantage); CI-E subsumes "asymmetric opportunity" and "hard prioritization (top 2-3)"; CI-F subsumes "uncomfortable truths surviving editing"; CI-G subsumes "time-bound actionable recommendations." The current rubric's "graceful degradation when data is missing" is best folded into CI-F's assumption-naming.

2. **One criterion the current rubric does not name but practitioners do:** the *industry-structure context*. BCG Advantage Matrix and Porter Five Forces both demand it. A separate criterion or an explicit sub-clause inside CI-E should test whether the brief locates the competitive move inside its industry structure (Volume / Stalemate / Fragmented / Specialisation; or Porter's force balance) before recommending.

3. **Real exemplars to anchor judge prose later:** Zuora subscription-economy narrative (CI-A, CI-C), CB Insights Apple/Google teardowns (CI-C structure), Netflix-vs-Blockbuster (CI-C trajectory + CI-F uncomfortable truth — Blockbuster's failure was named at the time and ignored), Stripe's developer-first counter-positioning to Square's SMB-POS (CI-D Helmer counter-positioning), McKinsey war-gaming output as competitor-response prediction (CI-E posture).

4. **What to deliberately not encode** (per rubric design hygiene, separate from this research's scope): no claims about specific competitors or industries that would bias the judge across fixtures; no statistical thresholds; no transferred meta-patterns from other lanes.
